Research

Boston University

I am working in the low-mass star group lead by Prof. P. Muirhead at Boston University. I am a member of the PINES (Perkins INfrared Exosatellite Survey) Collaboration. PINES is a search for transiting exosattelites (exoplanets or exomoons, depending on the classification of the host) around L and T type dwarfs.

L-type and T-type dwarfs are unique objects; depending on their mass they can be classified as hydrogen fusing stars, brown dwarfs, or free-floating planets. All of these objects have masses ranging from 0.005 to 0.080 solar masses and have radii roughly equal to that of Jupiter. Because L and T dwarfs span the gap between these classifications, they are unique laboratories to help us learn more about the similarities and differences in the formation, convection, and angular momentum evolution of all three object types.

L and T type dwarfs are known to be photometrically variable, and it is believed that early L objects variability is caused by magnetic spots on the surface, while late L and T type dwarfs variabilty is caused by a cloudy atmosphere. The exact transition from magnetic to cloudy variability is not known, and will be the focus of my project.

The American Museum of Natural History

In the summer of 2019, I had the privilege of completing an REU with the Brown Dwarfs in New York City (BDNYC) group at the American Museum of Natural History (AMNH). Here, I worked with Prof. Kelle Cruz, Dr. Jackie Faherty, and Dr. Johanna Vos to determine the rotation rates of 26 brown dwarfs from the Kepler 2 mission. Typically, brown dwarfs and low mass stars are observed in the infrared, since that is the wavelength that the objects are brightest in. We wanted to see if it was possible to detect variability, and therefore rotation rates, using optical observations, and determine how the lightcurves compared to their infrared counterparts. Out of the 26 objects, I was able to detect two rotation rates, both of which matched previously detected rotation rates in the infrared. For one of these objects, I was able to determine the light curve evolution. I presented this research at the American Astronomical Society winter meeting in 2020 and was awarded a Chambliss Astronomy Achievement Award Student Prize for my presentation.

The University of Alabama

From August 2017 to May 2020, I worked with Dr. Jeremy Bailin to create Color-Magnitude Diagrams (CMDs) of galaxies in the MUGS simulations- a set of 16 Milky Way like galaxies simulated using N-body smoothed particle hydrodynamics at high resolution. During the first two semesters that I worked on the project, I created over 10,000 synthetic images of these simulated galaxies and created the CMDs for each. We use these CMDs to compare the MUGS galaxies to MilkyWay like galaxies observed by the Sloan Digital Sky Survey. I have had the opportunity to present this research multiple times, both as an oral presentation for RRS Live, an online broadcasted research symposium, in poster presentations at URCA (Undergraduate Research and Creative Activity Conference), and CUWiP. I have also been a mentor to undergraduate students taking over this project as I transitioned to graduate school.

Green Bank Observatory

In the summer of 2018, I worked with Dr. Andrew Seymour studying Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs). I analyzed Arecibo Telescope data of FRB110523 with the hope of detecting a repeat burst. I detected one potential burst candidate which was then used in a successful proposal for follow-up observations. I presented this research at the American Astronomical Society winter meeting in 2019, as well as at CUWiP. It has since been determined that FRB110523 is not a repeating FRB.
